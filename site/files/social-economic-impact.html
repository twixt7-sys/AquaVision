Social & Economic Impact

social-economic-impact v1.0.0 updated 2026-07-17 ?Assumption ◆Confidence: low raw JSON ↗

Note

Impact claims are hypotheses until measured. This file states the mechanisms and, for each, what evidence would be needed to substantiate it. An impact claim without a measurement plan is aspiration.

Economic Impacts

Impact
Reduced catastrophic income volatility for aquaculture households
Mechanism

Earlier warning and response capacity reduce the frequency or severity of harvest-destroying kill events.

Who Benefits
Operators — most acutely smallholders, for whom a kill is existential.
Citations
inquirer-taal-fishkill-2023 pna-tilapia-fishkill
Evidence Needed

A matched comparison of income volatility between monitored and unmonitored farms across multiple cycles. See methodology.json — this is the load-bearing RQ5.

Honest Caveat
Directly limited by the affordability paradox and by whether farms can act on warnings.
Impact
Improved production efficiency (FCR, survival, yield)
Mechanism

Condition-aware feeding and earlier intervention improve the production metrics that determine profitability.

Citations
innovasea-fcr-guide globalseafood-fcr-primary-indicator
Evidence Needed

Measured FCR and survival differences, controlled for confounders. The chronic sub-lethal hypothesis (cost-of-inaction.json) is the key untested driver here.

Impact
Access to credit and insurance
Mechanism

Continuous, verifiable environmental records turn an unquantified, uninsurable asset into an insurable one, potentially unlocking finance for the currently excluded.

Who Benefits
Smallholders and cooperatives most, because they are the most credit-excluded.
Evidence Needed

An insurer or lender actually pricing against the data. Requires loss history we do not have. Chicken-and-egg.

Honest Note
Potentially the largest long-run economic impact — possibly larger than the direct loss prevention — but the most speculative and the slowest to materialise.
Impact
Reduced monitoring labour cost
Mechanism
Automates the manual monitoring round.
Magnitude
Modest monetarily; the labour is cheap. The safety benefit (below) matters more.

The Distributional Question

Statement
Who actually captures the benefit determines whether the impact is positive or regressive.
The Risk

If the product primarily serves well-capitalised commercial farms, it improves their competitive position against smallholders — a regressive outcome that widens inequality in the sector. Precision technology defaults to entrenching the advantage of those who can afford it.

The Commitment

The cooperative and institutional channels are the mechanism for equitable distribution, and they are in the plan from the start precisely to counter this default. Whether they succeed is the difference between a product that reduces inequality and one that deepens it.

The Honest Admission

We cannot guarantee the equitable outcome. We can commit to pursuing it actively and to measuring who actually benefits, rather than assuming the benefit is evenly distributed. See ethics-data-privacy.json#/equity_and_extraction.

worldbank-disease-risk-2014

Measuring Impact Honestly

Principle
Impact must be measured against a counterfactual, not asserted from activity.
The Trap

Counting drones deployed or profiles collected is measuring ACTIVITY, not IMPACT. Impact is the difference the activity made against what would have happened anyway.

The Method

See methodology.json. The stepped-wedge design and neighbouring-farm comparison are how impact gets measured without the unethical withholding design. Impact claims in this file remain hypotheses until that measurement is done.

The Commitment

Publish the impact evaluation including null results. If monitored farms do not do better than comparable unmonitored ones, that is a finding the field needs, and burying it would be both dishonest and a disservice to every other team considering this approach.

The Impact Thesis In One Paragraph

Statement

In a sector where a single night can destroy a family's year of income, where the people most exposed are the least equipped to see it coming, and where the dangerous work of watching the water falls on someone in a small boat before dawn — a platform that makes the water legible, at depth, at scale, at low marginal labour cost, has the potential for genuine social and economic good. That potential is real, it is bounded by affordability and by adoption, it carries second-order risks of intensification and inequality, and it is entirely unproven. Stated that way, it is both honest and, for the right impact investor or thesis committee, compelling.
