Airframe & Propulsion

airframe-propulsion v1.0.0 updated 2026-07-17 ◎Target ◆Confidence: low raw JSON ↗

Status Note

Design targets only. No airframe exists. Component masses are placeholders pending a real vendor survey.

Configuration

Type
Quadrotor, X-configuration, ducted rotors
Source of Geometry

The supplied render shows four shrouded rotors, a central fuselage, twin ventral pods, a forward gimbal, and a sprawled quadrupod gear with wide feet.

Render Caveat

The render is an industrial design study. Rotor diameter, arm length, and pod volume in the image have not been validated against this specification. Do not scale from the image.

Why A Quadrotor

Considered Alternatives
Option
Hexarotor
Pro

Motor-out redundancy — genuinely important over water where a failure means loss of aircraft.

Con
More mass, more cost, more drag.
Verdict

Should be seriously reconsidered. Over water, with no forced-landing option, single-motor-failure survivability is worth real mass. The render shows four rotors; the safety case may demand six. Do not let the industrial design decide the airframe configuration.

Option
Fixed-wing VTOL
Pro
Far better endurance and range — the thing we are worst at.
Con
Cannot hover efficiently, and hovering is what the winch requires. Disqualifying.
Verdict
Rejected. Our mission is hover-dominated by construction.
Option
Coaxial quad (X8)
Pro
Redundancy in a compact footprint.
Con
Coaxial rotors lose efficiency to each other's wake.
Verdict
Worth evaluating against the hexarotor.
The Decision

Quadrotor as the baseline because the render specifies it and it is the lightest configuration. FLAGGED FOR REVIEW: over-water operation with no forced-landing site is a strong argument for rotor redundancy, and this decision was made on aesthetics rather than on a safety analysis. Revisit before committing to tooling.

Ducted Rotors

Why

Primarily safety. This machine operates near people on narrow dikes and small boats, and a shroud means a person cannot contact a blade.

Requirement Id
REQ-HW-010
Secondary Benefits
  • Marginal static thrust improvement from a well-designed duct
  • Protects the rotor from the tether — non-trivial, since we are flying with a line hanging beneath us
  • Reads as benign, which matters for social licence
Costs
  • Mass — ducts are heavy and they are 1.35 kg of the airframe budget
  • Drag in forward flight
  • Increased wind sensitivity: a duct is a sail, and this aircraft already has a wind problem
The Honest Trade

Ducts cost endurance and worsen crosswind handling on an aircraft whose wind envelope is already its weakest attribute. They are retained because the safety case and the brand both want them. That is a defensible choice made knowingly. What would NOT be defensible is a later weight-saving exercise quietly deleting them without re-running the safety analysis that justified them.

Flag

If the mass budget must be recovered, the ducts are the largest single target and the one that must NOT be cut casually.

Propulsion Targets

Motors
4
Thrust To Weight Target
2
Thrust To Weight Rationale

2:1 at MTOM is the conventional minimum for adequate control authority in wind. Below 1.8:1 the aircraft cannot recover from a gust while station-keeping, and station-keeping in wind is the entire profiling mission.

Required Total Thrust N
approximately 133 N at 6.8 kg MTOM and 2:1
Disc Loading Target

Low. Larger rotors, lower disc loading, better hover efficiency and endurance — at the cost of a physically bigger aircraft.

Disc Loading Note

This is the central airframe trade. Hover efficiency improves as disc loading falls, and our mission is hover-dominated, so the physics pushes hard toward large rotors. Against that: a bigger aircraft is harder to dock, harder to transport, more wind-exposed, and heavier. The render's proportions suggest a moderate disc loading that has not been optimised against anything.

Propellers
Folding, for dock stowage. Field-replaceable without tools.
Esc
Redundant power feed. An ESC failure over water is a lost aircraft.

Stiffness-to-mass. Arm flex introduces vibration into the flight controller and blur into the imagery.

Material
Moulded polymer, white
Why

White is a thermal decision in a tropical deployment as much as an aesthetic one. Internal temperature rise degrades battery life and sensor accuracy, and a black airframe sitting on a dock at noon in the Philippines is an oven.

Component
Fasteners
Material
Marine-grade stainless or titanium
Why

Galvanic corrosion in a brackish environment eats steel. This is a lesson every marine drone programme learns expensively.

Component
Landing gear
Material
Composite, sprawled quadrupod, wide feet
Why

The render shows wide feet and a broad stance — correct for soft mud dikes and pitching boat decks. Good industrial design instinct.

Sealing

IP54 minimum. Conformal coating on all boards regardless of enclosure rating, because enclosure seals fail and the tropics find every failure.

The Ditching Provision

Principle
Assume it goes in the water. Design for it rather than pretending it will not happen.
Requirement Id
REQ-HW-004
Elements
Element
Positive buoyancy
Spec
The aircraft floats, inverted or upright, with the payload attached.
Mass Cost Kg
0.14
Element
Locator beacon
Spec
Independent power, 4 hours minimum, audible and RF.
Element
Lithium containment
Spec

A submerged lithium pack in a food-production water body is an environmental incident, not just a lost asset. Containment is not optional and it is not primarily about protecting the battery.

Element
Water-aware failsafe
Spec

A conventional RTH assumes a land home point. Over a lake, a naive failsafe flies a dying aircraft toward a shore it cannot reach and ditches in deep water instead of on the nearest bank. The failsafe logic must know it is over water and must know where the reachable land is.

Why This Is A First Class Requirement

Marine drone programmes that treat ditching as an edge case lose aircraft and then discover the environmental and reputational cost afterwards. In a lake used for food production, with a community watching, a sunk drone with a lithium pack is a story. Budget the mass now.

Corrosion

Brackish exposure assumed even at freshwater sites — spray, sweat, and the dock's own salt environment near the coast.

Uv

Continuous tropical UV degrades polymers. Material selection matters for a machine that lives outdoors.

The Thermal Problem

The dock sits in the sun. The aircraft sits in the dock. Ambient inside a closed dock at noon can exceed the battery's safe storage temperature, which shortens pack life substantially and silently. The dock needs ventilation or shading, and this is a dock requirement generated by an airframe constraint — the kind of cross-subsystem coupling that gets missed when subsystems are designed independently.
