Business Model Canvas

Citation Ids

  • osterwalder-bmc-2010

Note

The nine-block canvas, filled honestly. Where a block rests on an untested assumption, it says so. A canvas full of confident assertions at TRL 3 is a work of fiction; this one flags its fictions.

Customer Segments

Primary

Commercial lake-cage and pond aquaculture operators with response capacity (aeration) and a budget line.

Secondary
Cooperatives aggregating smallholders; BFAR/LGU as programme buyers.
Explicitly Deferred

Individual smallholders — the greatest need, no direct affordability. Reached via cooperatives or public programmes, not direct sales.

Segmentation Axis
Response capacity, not scale. See customer-personas.json#/segmentation_logic.
Assumption Flag

That this segment exists in sufficient density on the target lakes and will pay. UNTESTED — Phase 0.

Value Propositions

Ranked By Verifiability
  • Lower aeration electricity at equal or better DO (checkable in one cycle, metered, already budgeted).
  • Self-compiling environmental records for certification audits (replaces existing manual labour).
  • Measurable FCR improvement via condition-aware feeding (arithmetic sourced, magnitude unproven).
  • Earlier detection of deteriorating water quality (core claim, hard to demo on demand).
  • Avoided mass mortality (most compelling, least demonstrable, undercut by our own weather limitation).
The Discipline

Sell top-down this list, not bottom-up. Lead with the checkable ROI. See value-proposition.json#/the_value_hierarchy.

Detail File
data/04_solution/value-proposition.json

Channels

Direct

Field sales to commercial farms — high-touch, relationship-led, slow. This sector does not buy from a website.

Cooperative
The route to smallholders and the best dock economics.
Institutional
BFAR/LGU programmes and development-finance-backed deployments.
Partner

Feed distributors (existing farm reach, but a structural conflict — we reduce their volume), aquaculture consultants (trusted gatekeepers), insurers/lenders (long game).

Assumption Flag

That cooperatives can levy members for a shared service. UNTESTED and load-bearing for the smallholder story.

Customer Relationships

Model

High-touch service, not self-serve SaaS. The customer is buying an outcome and a relationship, and the relationship must survive the sector's memory of vendors who left.

Retention Logic

Value compounds with data duration — each cycle starts smarter. This is the argument for a subscription and for low churn once trust is established.

The Trust Problem

Distrust of technology vendors is probably the biggest commercial obstacle in the whole plan, larger than any engineering problem. Earned honestly by the sector's history. Overcome only by demonstrated outcomes and a credible answer to 'what happens when you leave'.

Revenue Streams

Detail File
data/10_business/pricing-revenue.json
Primary Model

Monitoring-as-a-service subscription, priced against loss avoidance and aeration savings rather than hardware cost.

Why Not Hardware Sales

Selling a USD 20k+ drone to a fish farm is a non-starter on affordability and on the sector's gadget-fatigue. Service pricing spreads the cost, aligns our incentive with uptime, and keeps the data relationship alive.

Possible Streams
  • Per-cycle or per-month monitoring subscription
  • Per-dock deployment fee
  • Dispensing service (treatment delivery)
  • Data/reporting for certification
  • Institutional programme contracts
  • NOT data monetisation — deliberately declined, see ethics-data-privacy.json

Key Resources

The Moat Such As It Is

Accumulated site-specific data (years of profiles on lakes nobody else measures), local regulatory relationships, and the winch/calibration engineering. NONE of which exist yet — the moat is currently a plan.

Physical
Drones, docks, per-site sondes.
Human
See team-structure.json — none hired.
Intellectual

The self-calibrating observation architecture. Not patent-hard; the real defensibility is data and relationships.

The Honest Note

At TRL 3 the key resource is the founding team's ability to execute Phase 0-3. Everything else is aspirational.

Key Activities

  • Hardware development (winch is the critical path)
  • Field validation over a full season
  • Regulatory pathway (BVLOS)
  • Building the coalition per site (five constituencies must each say yes)
  • Model training on real data
  • Service delivery at scale — becomes the biggest activity

Cost Structure

Detail File
data/10_business/unit-economics.json
Major Lines
  • Hardware (drone + dock + per-site sonde)
  • Field operations and maintenance labour — the biggest line at scale, routinely underestimated
  • Battery opex (a consumable, not capital)
  • R&D through validation
  • Regulatory and certification
  • Cloud/platform
The Structural Challenge

This is a hardware + field-service business, not a software business. It does not have software margins and it should not be pitched as if it does. The cost to serve is real, recurring, and geographically distributed.

Assumption Flag

Every cost line rests on a BOM with zero real quotes and a maintenance burden that is estimated, not measured. See risk R-14.

The Canvas Verdict

Coherence

The blocks fit together: service revenue matches a distributed cost structure, cooperative channels match smallholder economics, and the value hierarchy matches the trust problem.

The Three Assumptions the Whole Canvas Rests On
  • That the target segment will pay (revenue).
  • That cooperatives can aggregate demand (channel + smallholder reach).
  • That BVLOS comes through (cost structure + flagship value).
The Honest Summary

This is a coherent business model built on three untested load-bearing assumptions. That is normal for TRL 3. What is not normal — and what this canvas does deliberately — is naming the three assumptions so they can be tested first, before the money is spent. A canvas that hides its assumptions behind confident prose is the more common and more dangerous artefact.
