Note

The nine-block canvas, filled honestly, for a company that is now BOTH a software business (Phase 1/2) and a hardware-plus-field-service business (Phase 3). Where a block rests on an untested assumption, it says so. A canvas full of confident assertions at this stage is a work of fiction; this one flags its fictions.

The Repositioning

Statement

AquaVision is no longer 'we build a drone that a farm eventually buys access to'. It is 'we build the aquaculture management platform; the drone and IoT sensors are how the platform sees and acts for farms that have outgrown manual logging'.

Why This Changes the Canvas

Every block below used to assume a single high-touch, high-cost hardware-service customer. That customer still exists, but now sits at the top of a funnel rather than at the front door. The segments, channels, relationships, revenue, and costs are each now genuinely two different businesses layered on one platform brand, and this canvas says so explicitly rather than blending them into one story that fits neither.

Customer Segments

Phase 1 Foundation
Who

Beginner and smallholder fish farmers establishing or running their first ponds — individuals, not institutions.

Role In the Model

The acquisition and data-accumulation engine. Free or near-free access removes the affordability barrier that the old hardware-first model could not solve for this exact group.

Why They Are Now Primary Not Deferred

Under the previous model, smallholders were explicitly deferred as unaffordable and reached only indirectly through cooperatives. Under this model they are the top of the funnel: a mobile app has near-zero marginal cost to serve, so the segment the mission most wants to help is also the segment the business can serve first and directly.

Phase 2 Monetization
Who

Growing commercial farms that have outgrown manual record-keeping and are willing to pay for analytics, predictive health monitoring, a marketplace, and expert access.

Role In the Model

The paying subscriber base. These are Phase 1 users who have scaled, plus commercial operators who enter directly at the premium tier.

Phase 3 Smart Aquaculture
Who

Large-scale and enterprise aquaculture operators — commercial lake-cage and pond operations, cooperatives, and institutional programme buyers (BFAR/LGU) with response capacity (aeration) and a hardware budget line.

Role In the Model

The hardware-as-a-service and enterprise dashboard audience. This is the segment the original hardware-first plan targeted as its only customer; it is now one of three, reached with a warmer pipeline because Phase 1/2 usage data and trust already exist before the hardware conversation starts.

Segmentation Axis

Farm maturity and data sophistication, not just scale — a smallholder with three ponds and a large enterprise operator both start on the same free app; what changes is which layer of the platform they need next.

Assumption Flag

That Phase 1 free usage converts to Phase 2 paid usage at a viable rate, and that Phase 2 paying farms are a credible warm lead source for Phase 3 hardware. Both UNTESTED. See unit-economics.json#/phase_2_monetization and go-to-market.json#/the_funnel_across_phases.

Value Propositions

Phase 1 Foundation
  • Free AI-assisted fish farming guide and setup assistant — removes the 'where do I even start' barrier for a first-time farmer.
  • Water quality logging by photo/manual entry, feeding schedules, and record keeping that replace a paper notebook at zero cost.
  • Photo-based disease identification — a triage tool, not a lab diagnosis, but instant and free.
  • A community of other farmers and learning modules — the social/trust layer a solo beginner otherwise has to build alone.
Phase 2 Monetization
  • Predictive health monitoring — machine learning trained on the water-quality and production data the farm already logged for free in Phase 1, so the model gets smarter with each cycle the farm stays subscribed.
  • Premium analytics and smart recommendations that turn raw logs into decisions (feed adjustment, stocking density, timing).
  • A marketplace connecting the farm to feed, equipment, and buyers — solves a real transaction-cost problem, not just an information one.
  • Paid access to aquaculturists and vets for consultation — the human expertise a smallholder alone cannot afford, now purchasable per-session.
Phase 3 Smart Aquaculture
  • Automated, continuous water-column and surface monitoring via IoT probes and Omni Drone integration — replaces the impossible manual round across many stations.
  • PondTwin digital-twin visualization for enterprise-scale operational awareness.
  • Fewer aerator-hours at equal or better dissolved oxygen and other checkable ROI claims — see value-proposition.json for the detailed, verifiability-ranked hierarchy built for this segment.
  • An enterprise dashboard rolling up fleet-wide data across sites.
The Discipline

Sell each phase's value on its own terms. Do not pitch Phase 1 users on hardware ROI they cannot afford, and do not pitch Phase 3 enterprise buyers on 'try it free' — they need the checkable, verifiable ROI case exactly as the original hardware plan argued. See value-proposition.json#/the_value_hierarchy for that discipline, which still applies unchanged to the Phase 3 sale.

Channels

Phase 1 Foundation
App Store Organic

App store search and organic discovery — the primary, near-zero-cost channel for a free consumer app.

Community Led

Existing fish-farming Facebook groups, forums, and farmer networks — word of mouth among people who already talk to each other about this exact problem.

Content Marketing

Educational content (guides, videos, disease-identification examples) that ranks and gets shared, doubling as the learning-modules product surface.

Institutional Partnership

Agricultural extension programmes and NGOs that already reach smallholders and can distribute the free app as a public good.

Phase 2 Monetization
In App Conversion

The primary channel is the product itself — usage-triggered upsell prompts once a free user's data and needs outgrow the free tier. No separate sales motion required for most conversions.

Phase 3 Smart Aquaculture
Direct Field Sales

Field sales to commercial farms — high-touch, relationship-led. This segment does not buy hardware deployment from an app store prompt.

Cooperative

The route to smallholder-adjacent hardware economics — a shared dock amortised across cooperative members.

Institutional
BFAR/LGU programmes and development-finance-backed deployments.
Partner

Feed distributors, aquaculture consultants, insurers/lenders — same partner logic as the original hardware plan, see go-to-market.json.

Assumption Flag

That organic/community channels can acquire Phase 1 users at low CAC without paid marketing spend, and that cooperatives can still levy members for a shared Phase 3 hardware service. Both UNTESTED.

Customer Relationships

Phase 1 2 Model

Self-serve, product-led. The relationship is mediated by the app: onboarding flows, in-app support, community moderation, and usage-triggered upgrade prompts, not a sales rep.

Phase 3 Model

High-touch service, not self-serve — unchanged from the original plan. The enterprise customer is buying an outcome and a relationship, and the relationship must survive the sector's memory of vendors who left.

Retention Logic

In Phase 1/2, value compounds with logging history — the predictive models get more useful the longer a farm stays, which is the retention argument for a subscription. In Phase 3, value compounds with data duration in the same way, but retention is additionally reinforced by hardware being installed and maintained by AquaVision, not the customer.

The Trust Problem

Distrust of technology vendors who over-promise and leave is still the biggest commercial obstacle in the Phase 3 hardware relationship, exactly as in the original plan. It is a smaller obstacle in Phase 1/2 because the ask (download a free app) carries none of the switching cost or capital exposure that made trust so central to a hardware sale.

Model
None. Free or near-free by design.
Why

This tier's job is acquisition and data accumulation, not revenue. Monetising it would undercut the affordability promise that makes it the acquisition engine in the first place.

Phase 2 Monetization
Model

Freemium subscription (premium tier, monthly/annual) + marketplace take-rate on transactions + consultation fees for paid expert access.

Streams
  • Premium subscription (analytics, predictive health, recommendations)
  • Marketplace take-rate (feed, equipment, buyer transactions)
  • Consultation fees (aquaculturist/vet access, per-session or revenue-share)
Phase 3 Smart Aquaculture
Model

Hardware-as-a-service and deployment fees — AquaVision retains ownership of drones, docks, and probes; the customer pays for the monitoring outcome, not the equipment.

Why Not Hardware Sales

Unchanged from the original plan: a five-figure capital ask to a fish farm is a non-starter on affordability and gadget-fatigue, and selling the hardware outright transfers maintenance responsibility to a customer who cannot maintain it, which destroys the data quality the whole product depends on.

Streams
  • Per-dock or per-site monitoring subscription
  • Deployment/installation fee
  • Institutional programme contracts
Explicitly Not A Stream
Data monetisation — deliberately declined at every phase. See ethics-data-privacy.json.
The Restructuring

This is the central change from the prior canvas: revenue is no longer a single hardware-service line. It is a software subscription-and-marketplace business (Phase 2) sitting on top of a free acquisition layer (Phase 1), with a hardware-as-a-service layer (Phase 3) added for the customers who need and can afford automated physical monitoring.

Key Resources

Phase 1 2
Software

The mobile/web app, the AI farming assistant, the disease-ID model, the community platform, and — critically — the accumulating corpus of farmer-logged water-quality and production data, which is both the Phase 2 ML training asset and a future Phase 3 sales asset.

Human

Product/engineering team, community management, aquaculture domain experts contracted for consultations. See team-structure.json — none hired yet.

Phase 3
Physical
Drones, docks, per-site sondes.
Intellectual

The self-calibrating observation architecture (winch/probe engineering) and the digital twin (PondTwin). Not patent-hard; the real defensibility is data and relationships.

The Moat Such As It Is

Accumulated site-specific and farm-specific data (starting from Phase 1 logging, long before any hardware is sold), local regulatory relationships, and the winch/calibration engineering. Almost none of which exists yet — the moat is currently a plan, and the Phase 1 app is the mechanism that starts building the data half of it cheaply.

The Honest Note

At this stage the key resource is the founding team's ability to ship a free app people actually use, then convert a credible fraction of them, then sell hardware into the warmest segment of that base. Everything else is aspirational.

Key Activities

  • Build and ship the Phase 1 app (the critical path — nothing else in this model works without a used product)
  • Grow and retain a free user base through community and content, not paid acquisition
  • Train and validate ML models (disease ID, predictive health) on farmer-logged data
  • Build and iterate the premium conversion funnel and marketplace
  • Hardware development and field validation over a full season (Phase 3, unchanged critical path from the original plan)
  • Regulatory pathway (BVLOS) for Phase 3 drone operations
  • Service delivery at scale for Phase 3 deployments — becomes the biggest activity once hardware customers exist

The Structural Honesty

AquaVision is now BOTH a software business (Phase 1/2) and a hardware-plus-field-service business (Phase 3) — not purely one or the other, and this canvas will not pretend otherwise in either direction. Phase 1/2 genuinely has software-business economics: low marginal cost per user, cost structure dominated by development, hosting, support, and customer acquisition. Phase 3 genuinely has hardware-business economics: it does not have software margins, and the cost to serve is real, recurring, and geographically distributed, exactly as the original hardware-first plan argued.

Phase 1 2 Major Lines
  • Product/engineering development (app, AI models, marketplace, platform)
  • Cloud hosting and inference compute — modest per-user, scales with active users
  • Customer support and community moderation
  • Customer acquisition cost — should be low if organic/community channels work as assumed, but unproven
  • Contracted aquaculture experts for the consultation marketplace
Phase 3 Major Lines
  • Hardware (drone + dock + per-site sonde)
  • Field operations and maintenance labour — the biggest line at scale, routinely underestimated
  • Battery opex (a consumable, not capital)
  • R&D through validation
  • Regulatory and certification
  • Cloud/platform (shared infrastructure with Phase 1/2, allocated)
Assumption Flag

Phase 1/2 cost assumptions rest on no real hosting/support cost data at scale yet. Phase 3 cost lines rest on a BOM with zero real quotes and a maintenance burden that is estimated, not measured, exactly as before. See risk register.

The Canvas Verdict

Coherence

The blocks fit together as two connected businesses on one brand: a free, self-serve software layer that generates users, data, and trust; a paid software layer that monetises that data and trust; and a hardware-as-a-service layer that monetises the customers who have outgrown software alone. Each layer's channel, relationship, and cost structure matches its own economics rather than being forced into one template.

The Assumptions the Whole Canvas Rests On
  • That Phase 1 can acquire and retain free users at low cost through organic/community channels alone.
  • That a meaningful fraction of those users convert to Phase 2 paid tiers (freemium conversion is the single least-tested number in this model).
  • That Phase 2's engaged, trusting user base becomes a genuinely lower-CAC pipeline into Phase 3 hardware sales, rather than two unrelated customer bases sharing a brand.
The Honest Summary

This is a coherent, two-layer business model built on three untested assumptions, the most fragile of which is the free-to-paid conversion rate — a number with no company-specific data behind it yet. That is normal for a pre-launch product. What is not normal, and what this canvas does deliberately, is naming that fragility instead of hiding it behind a confident subscriber-growth chart. A canvas that assumes its funnel converts is the more common and more dangerous artefact.
