FAQ & Objection Handling

faq-objections v2.0.0 updated 2026-07-18 ◎Target raw JSON ↗

Note

The hard questions, with honest answers. An objection-handling document that only contains softballs is useless. Rebuilt for the phased platform: Phase 1 (free app) and Phase 2 (premium) objections come first because they are now the sharpest business-model questions; the Phase 3 (hardware) objections that used to be the whole document are kept, largely intact, because they remain legitimate and the engineering hasn't changed. The answers concede what is true and defend what is defensible — a defended overclaim is worse than a conceded weakness.

Objections

phase1-2

Q
Why would a free app ever turn into a real business?
Difficulty
highest — this is now the sharpest business-model objection
Honest Answer

It might not, and that's the honest starting point. The logic is land-and-expand: a free tier removes the only real barrier a smallholder has — money — and gets us thousands of farmers logging real water and production data, which does two things a paid-only launch can't: it builds the training data our premium predictive models need, and it builds trust before we ever ask for a subscription. But 'the logic works on a whiteboard' is not evidence. We have zero users today, and therefore zero measured conversion rate, zero measured retention, and zero measured willingness-to-pay for the premium tier. Phase 1 exists specifically to find out whether farmers who download a free water-quality app ever come back, and whether any meaningful fraction of them upgrade. If they don't, we've learned that cheaply — before building a single drone.

What Not To Do

Don't cite freemium conversion benchmarks from other software categories as if they transfer to smallholder aquaculture. They don't obviously transfer, and claiming they do is the overclaim this whole document exists to avoid.

Source
business-model-canvas.json, pricing-revenue.json

phase1-2

Q
How do you actually convert free users into paying ones?
Difficulty
high
Honest Answer

The plan is need-triggered, not push-triggered: a farm that outgrows manual logging — enough ponds or cages that a notebook stops working, or a first scare from a disease outbreak the free tier flagged too late — is the moment predictive alerts and marketplace access become worth paying for, not a discount popup on day three. Candidate levers are a cap on free logging history, gated predictive features, and marketplace or consultation access that's paid by design because a third party is getting paid too. None of this is validated. We don't have a conversion funnel to show you because we don't have users to run one on yet — this is a Phase 1 measurement question, not a Phase 1 assumption we get to skip.

Source
pricing-revenue.json, go-to-market.json

phase1-2

Q
Why would a farmer trust an app from a company with no track record and nothing shipped?
Difficulty
high
Honest Answer

They probably won't, at first, and we don't get to assume otherwise. This sector has been burned by tech vendors who oversold and left — the same trust problem the hardware pitch always had, now facing a stranger's app instead of a stranger's drone. Our answer is the same discipline this document uses everywhere else: free removes financial risk but not trust risk, so we lead with narrow, checkable claims (a disease-ID suggestion with a confidence flag, never a diagnosis), make data exportable and ownership explicit from the first screen, and grow through cooperatives and farming communities where a trusted local voice vouches for us, rather than cold app-store acquisition. None of that substitutes for the app actually working for real farmers over real seasons, which hasn't happened yet.

Source
go-to-market.json

phase1-2

Q
Isn't this just another farm-management app? There are already dozens.
Difficulty
medium-high
Honest Answer

There are general farm-management and record-keeping apps, yes, and most are crop-first or livestock-first with aquaculture bolted on, if it's there at all. Our claim to differentiation is narrower than 'better app': aquaculture-specific disease identification and water-chemistry logging built around pond and cage realities, and a real upgrade path — premium analytics trained on the farmer's own logged history, then IoT sensors and a drone for operations that outgrow all of it. A generic farm app has no reason to build that last mile; we do, because it's where our hardware engineering already lives. That said, if a well-funded incumbent farm-management app adds aquaculture-specific features and undercuts us on price or reach, that's a real competitive threat, not a hypothetical one, and depth plus speed is our only answer to it right now.

Source
competitive-landscape.json

phase1-2

Q
If the app is free, who pays for building it, let alone the drone?
Difficulty
medium-high
Honest Answer

Right now: investors, in the same milestone-gated way the hardware-only ask used to work. The app is dramatically cheaper to build and iterate on than the drone, which is the point of sequencing it first — the initial round funds Phase 1 (build and launch the free app, prove activation) at a fraction of the hardware program's cost, with Phase 3 engineering continuing in parallel at low burn rather than absorbing the entire early spend. Phase 2 subscription and marketplace revenue is what's meant to fund most of Phase 3 eventually; until Phase 2 has real paying customers, that's a modeled assumption, not a schedule we can promise.

Source
funding-ask.json, financial-projections.json

phase1-2

Q
Doesn't going free-first just mean it takes longer to find out if any of this works?
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

It's worth stating the counter-argument fairly: yes, a phased plan has more steps before the hardware gets validated than 'raise a big round and build the drone first.' But each software step is measured in weeks of app usage, not a multi-season flight-validation programme, so the cheap, fast-to-falsify assumptions (will anyone use this, will anyone pay) get tested before the expensive, slow-to-falsify one (does the hardware work in the field) rather than after it. If Phase 1 shows nobody comes back to the app, we've saved ourselves from building expensive hardware for a company that can't acquire customers — that's a faster failure, not a slower one.

Source
milestones-roadmap.json

phase1-2

Q

Smallholders used to be 'too poor to afford this.' Did making the app free actually solve that, or is it a dodge?

Difficulty
high
Honest Answer

It solves the access problem, not the whole problem. A smallholder can now use real AI-assisted guidance, disease identification, and record-keeping without paying anything, which a hardware-only model genuinely couldn't offer her. What it doesn't solve: whether a free product changes her outcomes without the capital to act on what it tells her, and whether 'free' quietly means she isn't really the customer — the premium and enterprise tiers are. We're building this as a genuine acquisition-and-utility layer, not a loss leader we resent supporting, but the real test is whether smallholders keep using it once the novelty wears off, and we don't have that data yet.

Source
customer-personas.json, ethics-data-privacy.json

phase1-2

Q
Can your app actually tell me what's wrong with my fish from a photo?
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

Not with certainty, and we won't sell it that way. Image classifiers for animal and plant disease are notoriously overconfident on images outside their training distribution — different water color, lighting, camera, or local species than whatever it was trained on. We plan to ship it as a triage suggestion carrying a confidence flag, with 'when in doubt, consult a vet or extension officer' as the default, never a diagnosis and never a certainty. It also hasn't been built or validated yet — this is a Phase 1 development item, not a shipped capability today.

Source
ai-ml-models.json

phase3

Q

Why does the hardware need to fly? A robotic boat has better endurance and no aviation regulator.

Difficulty

highest — the sharpest technical objection, though it now surfaces later in the sales cycle since Phase 3 is an upgrade sold into an existing platform user, not a cold pitch

Honest Answer

A USV genuinely beats us on endurance and weather tolerance, and for some customers it's the right answer — we'll say so. Our advantages are three: transit speed across many stations (a boat crawls; we cover a lake array fast), simultaneous aerial imaging a surface craft physically cannot do, and total independence from the water's topology — we ignore dikes, nets, and cage structure that entangle a boat. If a customer values endurance over those three, the USV wins and we won't pretend otherwise. Our Phase 3 gate explicitly requires us to be willing to hear that a USV is the right architecture.

What Not To Do

Don't dismiss the USV. Conceding its strengths is what makes the rest of the answer credible.

Source
competitive-landscape.json#/competitor_categories (usv-asv)

phase3

Q
A fixed sensor buoy is a tenth of the price. Why would anyone buy the drone?
Difficulty
high
Honest Answer

For a single small pond, a buoy IS the right answer and we'll tell you that. Our economics beat a buoy when you need many stations at many depths many times a day — the lake cage array case — because a buoy can't profile the water column without a costly vertical sensor string, and depth is where the killing happens. Also, counterintuitively, our probe fouls LESS than a moored buoy, because it lives in a dry dock and gets rinsed between sorties rather than sitting in warm eutrophic water growing biofilm. But honestly, the strongest configuration is a few buoys for continuity plus a drone for depth and coverage — a hybrid — and that's where our roadmap points.

Source
competitive-landscape.json#/competitor_categories (fixed-sensor-buoys)

phase3

Q

You can't fly in a storm, but storms are what cause the fish kills. So the drone can't prevent the thing you're selling against.

Difficulty
high — and it's correct
Honest Answer

That's true, and it's why we don't lead with kill-prevention. We can't fly in the storm that triggers an overturn. What we CAN do is measure the loaded gun in the days before — the stratification and the oxygen debt building up — so the farmer acts before the trigger arrives. And a fixed buoy that keeps reporting through the storm is a genuine complement here. Our honest positioning is continuous management and pre-event warning, not storm-response. Anyone who sells you storm-response with a small multirotor is lying about physics.

Source
risk-register-fmea.json (R-11), use-cases.json#/uc-03-stratification-watch

phase3

Q
Won't the drone spread disease between farms?
Difficulty
medium-high
Honest Answer

Yes, if designed carelessly — a probe dipped in one pond and then another is a pathogen vector. So we engineered against it: each site gets its own dedicated sonde (the drone travels, the probe doesn't), the dock decontaminates the sonde AND the full tether length between sites, and every sonde's movement is logged so an outbreak can be traced. We also disclose the residual risk — we can't fully decontaminate the airframe at operating cadence. We treat this as a first-class hardware requirement, not an operational footnote, because it's the strongest objection to a multi-site probe drone.

Source
biosecurity-protocol.json

phase3

Q
Can you actually predict fish kills?
Difficulty
medium — and the tempting wrong answer is 'yes'
Honest Answer

Not yet, and we won't claim it until we've validated it against real events across a full season — which takes at least a year you can't compress. What we do today is show the MECHANISM in plain language: 'the bottom three metres are anoxic and rain is forecast tonight.' An experienced farmer can act on that better than on a black-box score, and it carries no false certainty. A false 'you're safe' from an unvalidated model could cost a harvest, so we'd rather show the measurement and let the expert judge.

Source
ai-ml-models.json#/models/kill-risk-forecast
Why This Matters

This is the question where overclaiming is most tempting and most dangerous. The honest answer is also the safer product.

phase3

Q
Can it measure algal toxins? Can it tell me the water is safe to harvest?
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

No. It measures chlorophyll and phycocyanin — pigments that tell you how much algae is present and whether it might be cyanobacteria, which SOMETIMES produce toxins. High readings are a reason to send a sample to a lab, never a diagnosis and never a clearance. People eat this fish, so we hold that line hard: the system never says 'safe'.

Source
problem-taxonomy.json (bio-toxin), risk-register-fmea.json (R-13)

phase3

Q
Your Phase 3 model assumes BVLOS. What if CAAP says no?
Difficulty
medium-high
Honest Answer

It's our biggest external dependency and we're engaging CAAP from Phase 0, not when we need something. If BVLOS is delayed or denied, we re-base the model on VLOS economics — worse, because a pilot per sortie erodes the labour-cost advantage, but not fatal for the higher-value use cases. What we won't do is carry an unresolved BVLOS assumption silently into a later round. It's on the critical path and we treat it that way.

Source
regulatory-framework.json, risk-register-fmea.json (R-03)

phase3

Q
Isn't there a risk this just helps farmers over-stock, making the problem worse?
Difficulty
medium — the ethical curveball
Honest Answer

Yes, that's a real second-order risk. Better instruments can let operators push density closer to the edge, and over-stocking is the root cause of the disaster we sell against. We have a partial answer — carrying-capacity analytics aimed at cooperatives and regulators who care about the whole lake — but we're honest that an individual operator with a profit motive might stock harder regardless. We'd rather name that tension than pretend the technology is purely benign. It's in our ethics chapter.

Source
ethics-data-privacy.json#/the_overstocking_paradox, risk-register-fmea.json (R-15)

phase3

Q
Can you even build the drone? A winched probe on a hovering aircraft sounds hard.
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

It IS the hard part, and it's our biggest technical risk — a swinging probe on a tether is a pendulum that couples into the flight controller, and it'll snag on nets. We de-risk it first, on a static gantry, 1000 cycles, before it ever flies. If it can't be made to work, the honest response is to change the vehicle — not to quietly drop the probe and sell a camera drone, which would have no defensible position. It's also exactly why it's our moat: anyone can fly a camera; calibrated depth chemistry from a hover is genuinely hard.

Source
probe-winch-subsystem.json

phase3

Q
The salmon-farming companies are way ahead of you and well funded.
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

They are, technically — they have underwater vision and biomass estimation we don't. But their whole cost structure is built for high-value salmon in cold, deep, well-capitalised marine cages, and it doesn't transfer to a tilapia cage worth a fraction as much. We compete on geography and economics, not on out-engineering them: the tropical, distributed, low-value-per-unit, smallholder-dense environment their cost structure can't reach. Our defence is local depth — regulatory relationships, lake-specific chemistry, cooperative channels, and now a software funnel they have no reason to build — not a technical lead we don't have.

Source
competitive-landscape.json#/competitor_categories (precision-aquaculture-platforms)

cross-phase

Q

What happens when you leave, like every other tech vendor did — or when I just delete the app?

Difficulty
medium — the farmer's real question
Honest Answer

Fair — this sector's been burned. Our answers are concrete: your data is yours, exportable, and stays with you, on the app or on a Phase 3 deployment alike. We train local technicians, not fly-in specialists. Procedures are documented in your language. Because we own and maintain the Phase 3 hardware as a service, our incentive is your uptime, not a one-time sale. For the free app specifically, deleting it costs you nothing, because you paid nothing, and your logged history exports on request rather than being held hostage to keep you subscribed. We'd rather you be able to leave us than be locked in — the lock-in that matters is that the product works, not that you're trapped.

Source
training-certification.json#/knowledge_transfer_and_exit

cross-phase

Q
What's your traction? Any users, any customers?
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

None on either layer, and that's the correct state for a company that hasn't spent on hardware before proving demand and hasn't shipped software before validating that farmers want it. Zero app downloads, zero premium subscribers, zero drones flown. Our first traction is a cheap Phase 0/1: a working free-app pilot with a defined cohort of real farmers, 25+ farmer interviews on the hardware side with real willingness-to-pay, and the payload and BVLOS groundwork. We'd rather show you honest zeros on both fronts than invented downloads or LOIs you'd catch in diligence.

Q
Show me the market size.
Difficulty
medium
Honest Answer

Two markets, two honest gaps. For the software layer, it's aquaculture operator counts at every scale, times a free-to-paid conversion rate and an ARPU I don't have measured yet. For the hardware layer, it's the same bottom-up operator-times-willingness-to-pay method as before, missing the BFAR operator counts and a measured willingness-to-pay. I won't multiply guesses into an authoritative-looking TAM for either. I'd rather show you exactly what Phase 0/1 resolves for each. If you want, I'll walk you through why the top-down 'industry-output-times-penetration' number that every agritech deck shows is meaningless.

The Meta Objection

Q

You're awfully honest about your weaknesses. Is that a strategy or are you just not confident?

Answer

It's a strategy, and a considered one. Every weakness I've named is discoverable in diligence — the unproven free-to-paid conversion, the no-track-record trust gap, the BVLOS dependency, the weather limitation, the affordability question the free tier only partly answers. Handing them to you with the mitigations attached costs me nothing you wouldn't find anyway, and it buys the one thing that actually matters in an early-stage bet: evidence that this team tells the truth about its own company. If the unverifiable claims are going to carry weight later, the verifiable honesty has to come first. Investors who want the hockey-stick will pass, and that's fine — they're not the right investors for a phased platform company with a real validation timeline on both its software funnel and its hardware.

Source
funding-ask.json#/the_counterintuitive_bet
