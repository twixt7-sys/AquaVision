Related Studies — Socioeconomics & Technology Adoption

studies-socioeconomic-adoption v1.0.0 updated 2026-07-17 ?Assumption ◆Confidence: low raw JSON ↗
ℹTHE HONEST STATE OF THIS COLLECTION
Statement:

This is the emptiest and most important collection in the research corpus, and the imbalance is diagnostic.

The Problem:

We have eight thematic collections. Seven are populated with real studies about sensors, algorithms, twins, and fish. This one — about whether anyone will actually adopt, pay for, and keep using the technology — is nearly empty because we did not search for it properly.

Why That Is Revealing:

The team's instinct was to research the technology, not the adoption. That instinct is exactly why aquaculture technology has a poor adoption record despite good technology. The engineering questions are tractable and interesting; the adoption questions are messy and decisive. We went where the light was.

The Correction:

Adoption literature for smallholder agricultural technology in developing economies is a large, mature field with decades of hard-won findings about why good technology fails. Almost none of it is in this repository. That is a genuine gap and it should embarrass us more than a missing sensor datasheet would.

Priority:

This collection deserves a dedicated literature search before Phase 1. It is more likely to change the plan than anything in the sensing literature.

Theme

socioeconomic_adoption

Phase Relevance

This collection was written against a drone-and-dock capital purchase, and its diagnostic — Rogers scores that product badly on four of five diffusion attributes — was a genuine problem for that pitch. Read against Phase 1 (a free or low-cost app aimed at smallholders as the acquisition tier of a phased platform) rather than a hardware purchase, the same Rogers framework reads far more favourably: compatibility and complexity improve sharply for a phone app versus a drone-and-dock installation, trialability improves sharply (an app can be tried and abandoned at zero cost, a dock cannot), and observability is still mixed but less lopsided. This is a genuinely more supportive read of the same citation, not a new claim — rogers-diffusion-2003's five attributes are simply being scored against a different product. The moore-crossing-chasm-1991 and worldbank-disease-risk-2014 findings (chasm risk, and the neediest-cannot-pay distributional trap) still apply and still matter for Phase 1: a free tier resolves the affordability half of that trap for acquisition, but conversion to Phase 2 paid tiers reintroduces it, and this collection's honest emptiness on smallholder adoption evidence has not been corrected merely by changing the product being scored against it.

Studies

Diffusion of innovations (2003)

Book!Challenges★HIGH — this framework should shape go-to-market

The foundational framework for how innovations spread through social systems: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability determine adoption rate.

Relevance To Omnidrone

The five attributes are a direct diagnostic on our product, and the result is uncomfortable.

The Diagnostic
Attribute
Relative advantage
Our Score
unknown
Why

We believe it is large. We have not measured it, and the farmer's twenty years of experience is a stronger incumbent than we credit.

Attribute
Compatibility
Our Score
poor
Why

Nothing about a drone fits an existing workflow. There is no budget line, no skill, no habit to attach to. We are asking for behaviour change, not a substitution.

Attribute
Complexity
Our Score
poor
Why

It is a drone, a dock, a probe, a cloud service, and a dashboard. Every one of those is a thing that can break in a way the farmer cannot fix.

Attribute
Trialability
Our Score
poor
Why

You cannot try a bit of it. The dock is a permanent installation. This is a major structural disadvantage that Rogers identifies as a strong predictor of slow adoption, and it argues hard for a service model over a sale.

Attribute
Observability
Our Score
mixed
Why

The drone flying is highly visible — good. The benefit is a kill that did not happen — invisible. We are visible in our costs and invisible in our value, which is precisely the wrong way round.

The Verdict

On Rogers' framework, this product scores badly on four of five attributes. That is not a reason to stop; it is a reason to design the go-to-market around the weaknesses. Trialability argues for a service model rather than a capital sale. Observability argues for leading with the aeration electricity saving, which IS observable in a monthly bill, rather than with prevented catastrophes, which are not.

Crossing the chasm: Marketing and selling high-tech products to mainstream customers (1991)

Book★MEDIUM

On the gap between early adopters and the pragmatic early majority in technology markets, and why products die in it.

Relevance To Omnidrone

The chasm framing predicts our specific failure mode: a well-capitalised demonstration farm adopts enthusiastically, generates a case study, and the pragmatic majority still does not buy — because the demonstration farm's conditions were not theirs.

The Application

Our company-profile.json commits to 'build for the median farm, not the flagship'. That commitment is precisely a chasm-crossing strategy, and it was arrived at instinctively rather than from this literature. Worth grounding it properly.

Reducing disease risk in aquaculture (2014)

Policy Report!Challenges★MEDIUM

Includes the finding that EMS/AHPND losses in the Mekong Delta fall mostly on small-scale producers who cannot afford them.

Relevance To Omnidrone

The distributional finding is the equity core of this venture and also its commercial trap. The people who need this most can afford it least. This is not a pitch problem to be solved with better framing; it is a structural feature of the market.

The Questions This Theme Must Answer

Note

These are the questions that decide whether the venture works. None of them are engineering questions, and none of them are currently answered.

Synthesis

The Uncomfortable Conclusion

The technical literature in this repository is reasonably well-mapped. The adoption literature is nearly absent. Yet on every honest reading of this project's risk profile, adoption is the more likely failure mode — the engineering problems are hard but tractable, while the adoption problems may be structural.

What the Thin Evidence We Have Says

Rogers' framework scores us badly on four of five adoption attributes. The one adoption-barrier finding we have names deployment cost and power supply — our two worst attributes. The one distributional finding says the neediest cannot pay. Every piece of adoption evidence in this collection argues against the venture, and there are only four pieces.

The Honest Recommendation

Before another sensor is specified, someone should spend two weeks reading smallholder technology adoption literature and two weeks talking to farmers. That month is more likely to change the plan — and to save the money — than any amount of winch engineering. This collection's emptiness is the most actionable finding in the research corpus, and it is a finding about us rather than about the field.
