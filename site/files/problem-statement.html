Problem Statement

problem-statement v2.0.0 updated 2026-07-18 raw JSON ↗

The Core Problem

One Sentence

Fish farmers at every scale are making decisions worth their livelihood with less information than the decision deserves — beginners because nobody ever taught them what to look for, growing farms because nobody built them a tool that scales past a notebook, and large operators because the water itself changes faster than any human can watch it.

Expanded

The problem is not one gap, it is a hierarchy of three. At the bottom, a first-time or smallholder farmer starts a pond or a cage with no structured guidance, no habit of logging anything, no easy way to identify a disease from a photo, and no community to ask before it is too late — so they learn by losing money. One tier up, a farm that survives its first few cycles and grows past a handful of units hits a different wall: the notebook that worked for one pond does not work for fifteen, there is no analytics layer to show which unit is underperforming and why, no predictive signal before a slow decline becomes a bad cycle, and no affordable way to reach an aquaculturist or vet who could look at the data and say something useful. At the top, the largest and most exposed operators — dense cage arrays in shared lakes — face the sharpest version of the same underlying problem: a water column that stratifies, deoxygenates, blooms, and overturns on timescales of hours, observed (where it is observed at all) at one point, at the surface, when someone happens to be there. Every documented mass mortality event sits at this top tier, and it is real and catastrophic. But it is the tip of a much larger iceberg — most farmers never reach the scale where that failure mode is even their biggest risk, because they fail earlier and more quietly, for lack of basic guidance.

Why It Persists

At the top tier, it persists because observation is expensive in labour, not in capital: profiling a lake cage array properly means a boat, a person, a sonde, and two hours, repeated several times a day including before dawn, at every cage, forever. Nobody does that. Further down the hierarchy it persists for a plainer reason: aquaculture extension services are thin and unevenly distributed, generic farm-management software is built for row-crop agriculture and does not know what a thermocline or an FCR is, and the knowledge that experienced farmers hold rarely gets written down, let alone digitised, in a form a beginner two provinces away can use. The industry has quietly settled into two different failure modes depending on where a farm sits on this hierarchy — beginners drop out or absorb repeated small losses they never diagnose, and large operators absorb the occasional catastrophic one — and almost nobody has built a single tool that meets a farmer where they are and grows with them.

The Problem Hierarchy

Note

This is the organising structure of the whole pitch and the direct rationale for a phased product rather than a single product. Each tier is a distinct problem with a distinct customer, a distinct willingness to pay, and a distinct feasible solution shape — conflating them is what the old, hardware-only framing did, and it is the mistake this rewrite exists to fix.

Tiers

The Guidance Gap — beginners and smallholders going in blind

Tier
1
Who

First-time and smallholder farmers, often financing a single pond or a handful of cages against a season's income, with no formal training and no digital tool built for their situation.

The Gap

No structured onboarding for farm setup and species/stocking decisions, no habit or easy method of logging water conditions or feeding, no accessible way to get a disease flagged from a phone photo, no peer community to ask a question before making an expensive mistake, and no record of any of it afterward to learn from.

Why It Matters

This is the largest population of farmers in the sector by headcount and the group the old hardware-first pitch had to explicitly defer on affordability grounds. They cannot pay for hardware and should not be sold it. What they need first is free or near-free software that replaces 'ask an experienced neighbour and hope' with structured guidance.

Addressed By

AquaVision App (Phase 1) — farm setup assistant, AI farming guidance, manual/photo water quality logging, feeding schedules, photo-based disease identification, community discussions, learning modules, digital record keeping.

Confidence
low
Honest Note
That this is farmers' actual top-of-mind problem, that a free app is the right form for the guidance, and that free users are reachable and will engage, are all untested. See stakeholder-pain-points.json and cost-of-inaction.json for the specific assumptions and how Phase 0/1 field data should test them.

The Scaling Gap — farms that outgrow manual tracking

Tier
2
Who

Farms that have survived their early cycles and grown into multiple ponds or cages, often with hired staff, that are still run on paper or spreadsheets.

The Gap

No analytics layer to compare units against each other or against the farm's own history, no predictive signal that flags a slow decline before it becomes a bad cycle, no affordable or geographically convenient access to an aquaculturist or vet, and no efficient marketplace to buy feed and equipment or find buyers — all of it done through personal networks and informal, often opaque, middlemen pricing.

Why It Matters

This is the tier where a farm has outgrown the tool that got it started but has not yet grown into the tier below, where automated hardware monitoring becomes economical. Left unserved, growth stalls on operational blindness rather than on capital or market demand.

Addressed By

AquaVision Premium (Phase 2) — analytics on the data logged in Phase 1, predictive health monitoring, smart recommendations, a feed/equipment/buyer marketplace, and paid expert consultations.

Provenance
?Assumption
Confidence
low
Honest Note
The entire premise of Phase 2 depends on Phase 1 having produced enough logged data to analyse, and on farms actually converting from free to paid once they feel this pain. Neither has happened yet — there are no users. This is a conversion-funnel hypothesis, not a measured behaviour.

The Coverage Gap — large operators who cannot see the water at the resolution it demands

Tier
3
Who

Commercial and enterprise cage/pond operations, typically dense arrays in shared lakes, for whom a mass mortality event is a real and recurring risk.

The Gap

Water quality is monitored at a spatial and temporal resolution orders of magnitude coarser than the processes that kill fish. This tier is where the pre-pivot problem statement lived in full, and it still holds — see 'the_reframe' below.

Why It Matters

This is the sharpest, most catastrophic expression of the observability problem, and the only tier where the economics of real-time automated monitoring hardware currently make sense — which is exactly why only the largest operators can afford it today, and why it is positioned as an upgrade layer rather than the entry point.

Addressed By

AquaVision Smart (Phase 3) — IoT sensors and water probes, Omni Drone integration, the PondTwin digital twin, and an enterprise dashboard.

Provenance
literature_validated_field_unvalidated
Confidence
medium
Honest Note
This tier carries the most external evidence of the three (see incident-case-studies.json) because it produces dramatic, reported events. That is a property of what gets reported, not proof that it is the sector's largest problem in aggregate — see cost-of-inaction.json's chronic-sublethal-loss and beginner-attrition categories for why the quieter tiers may matter more in total, even though they are harder to evidence.
Why A Single Hierarchy and Not Three Separate Pitches

The tiers are not three unrelated markets; they are one farmer's likely path, and the platform's argument for existing at all is that it can follow a farmer up that path — acquire them for free at tier 1, earn revenue as they grow into tier 2, and sell hardware once they reach tier 3 — rather than forcing them to find three different vendors. Whether that path is actually walked by real farmers, at what rate, and over what timescale, is the single biggest unproven assumption in this repository and is treated as such throughout.

The Reframe

Note

This is the intellectual centre of the tier-3 pitch specifically — the argument for why hardware, once a farm can afford it, should be a drone rather than more fixed sensors. It is not an argument for why hardware should be the company's starting point, which is the mistake the old framing made.

Conventional Framing
Fish farms need better sensors.
Why That Framing Is Wrong

Sensors are not the bottleneck. A multiparameter sonde is a commodity. Fixed sensor buoys already exist and are already sold into aquaculture. The bottleneck is that a fixed sensor measures one point in a system whose defining property is spatial and vertical heterogeneity — and covering an array of thirty cages with fixed sondes at every relevant depth is an absurd capital and maintenance proposition.

Our Framing

The problem at this tier is not sensing. It is COVERAGE — moving a good sensor to many places, at many depths, many times a day, at near-zero marginal labour cost. That is a logistics problem with an aerial answer.

The Consequence

This reframe is what makes an aerial platform the right shape of solution rather than an expensive novelty, for the farms that have reached this tier. If the problem were 'we need a DO reading', a $200 buoy wins and the drone is theatre. Because the problem is 'we need forty DO profiles a day across a lake without putting a person in a boat before dawn', the economics invert — for an operator large enough for that arithmetic to apply. That is a minority of farmers, which is precisely why this cannot be the whole company.

The Observability Gap

Id
prob-observability
Phase
3
Statement

Water quality is monitored at a spatial and temporal resolution orders of magnitude coarser than the processes that kill fish.

Evidence Summary

Fish kill investigations in Philippine lakes repeatedly attribute mortality to dissolved oxygen depletion and to ammonia and hydrogen sulfide following water column mixing — processes that develop over hours to days and are invisible to periodic surface sampling.

Vertical Blindness

Id
prob-blindness-at-depth
Phase
3
Statement

Surface measurements cannot detect stratification, and stratification is the precondition for the most catastrophic failure mode.

Expanded

A stratified lake in the hours before an overturn can present a perfectly reassuring surface: warm, oxygenated, clear. The killing agent is sitting three metres down, anoxic and sulfide-laden, waiting for a cold rain to bring it up. A surface sensor will report 'nominal' right up to the moment of the kill, and then report the kill.

Severity
critical
Addressed By
  • reeled probe vertical profiling
  • stratification index
  • oxygen debt index
Note

This is the strongest single technical argument for the reeled probe over every fixed-surface or imagery-only competitor at the tier-3 scale. Lead with it once a prospect has qualified into that tier.

Diel Sampling Bias

Id
prob-diel-sampling-bias
Phase
3
Statement

Manual sampling happens during working hours, which systematically measures the daily oxygen maximum and misses the pre-dawn minimum where mortality occurs.

Expanded

This is not a small error. It is a structural bias that makes the measurement anti-correlated with the risk it is supposed to detect. A farm sampling at 09:00 and finding 7 mg/L may be hours away from a 2 mg/L pre-dawn crash — and the data says everything is fine.

Severity
high
Addressed By
  • autonomous dock-based scheduling
  • pre-dawn sortie capability

Data Fragmentation and Loss

Id
prob-data-fragmentation
Phase
1_and_3
Statement

Where data is collected, it lives in paper logbooks and personal memory, is not analysed, does not accumulate, and dies with the notebook.

Unmeasured Environmental Externality

Id
prob-environmental-externality
Phase
3
Statement

Aquaculture effluent drives eutrophication of the shared water body, which harms the next farm and eventually the farm that caused it — but nobody measures it, so nobody manages it.

Expanded

This is a commons problem with an information vacuum at its centre. In a shared lake, each operator's stocking decision imposes oxygen demand on everyone. Without measurement there is no basis for allocating carrying capacity, so regulation is either absent or arbitrary, and the lake degrades until it kills everyone's fish at once — which is, empirically, what happens.

Severity
medium
Addressed By
  • multi-farm data
  • carrying capacity analytics
Note

This is the strongest public-good argument for the platform and therefore the strongest argument for government and development-finance interest. It is also the one that requires multi-farm data, which collides directly with farmer data sensitivity. That tension is real and unresolved — see ethics-data-privacy.json.

Who Bears the Cost

Party
Beginner and first-time farmers
Burden

The largest population by headcount and the primary casualty of the guidance gap. Their losses are individually small and rarely reported, but they are frequent enough, and discouraging enough, that they plausibly represent the sector's single largest source of attrition — nobody has measured this, which is itself part of the problem. See cost-of-inaction.json.

Party
Smallholder cage operators
Burden

Most exposed to the catastrophic tier, least equipped to absorb it. A single kill event can wipe out a year of income and the debt taken to finance the cycle. They are also the least able to buy a hardware solution — which is why they are served by the free Phase 1 tier, not sold Phase 3 equipment, and that resolution must not be papered over.

Party
Growing / mid-scale farms
Burden

Have outgrown manual tracking but have not reached the scale where automated sensor hardware is economical. Their losses are the quiet, uncounted cost of decisions made without analytics, predictive signal, or expert input — real, but invisible in any published statistic.

Party
Commercial farms
Burden

Larger absolute losses at the catastrophic tier, more capacity to absorb, more capacity to pay. The realistic first paying customer for Phase 3 hardware.

Party
Feed and fingerling suppliers
Burden
Demand collapses after a regional kill event; carry credit exposure to farms that fail.
Party
Local economies
Burden
Fish kills in lake economies are employment shocks, not just business losses.
Party
Consumers
Burden
Supply shocks and price volatility.
Party
The water body itself
Burden
Bears the cumulative nutrient load; has no representation in any operator's decision.

Counterarguments We Take Seriously

Objection
Farmers already know when their water is bad. They have decades of experience.
Our Response

Largely true of experienced operators, and the product must respect it. Experienced operators read weather, wind, and fish behaviour well. But intuition cannot see a thermocline shoaling three metres down, cannot be awake at every cage at 04:00, and does not exist yet in a farmer on their first cycle. The claim is not that instruments beat experience — it is that instruments extend it into the depths and hours where experience has no access, and that guided software can compress the years it normally takes to build that experience in the first place.

Residual Risk

If experienced operators judge the information redundant, willingness to pay collapses at tier 3. If beginners judge an app redundant to asking a neighbour, acquisition collapses at tier 1. Both are genuine risks and it is what farmer discovery in Phase 0 must test honestly, including by actively seeking farmers who say no.

Objection

Information is useless without the capacity to respond. A warning at 3 a.m. to a farmer with no emergency aeration just tells them what they are about to lose.

Our Response

This is the most serious objection to the tier-3 hardware business and we do not have a complete answer. It is why the dispensing subsystem matters: the platform must be able to deliver a response, not only a warning. It is also why the value proposition must be tested against farms that HAVE response capacity first.

Residual Risk
High. Listed as a kill criterion in the Phase 0 gate for exactly this reason.
Objection
A fixed sensor buoy is a tenth of the price.
Our Response

For one point, yes, and for a single small pond a buoy may genuinely be the right answer — we should say so rather than sell a drone. The economics favour the aerial platform when the number of stations times the number of depths times the sampling frequency is large, which is the lake cage array case. Below that threshold we are the wrong product, and pretending otherwise poisons the well.

Residual Risk
Medium. Requires disciplined qualification of prospects.
Objection
The drone will spread disease between farms.
Our Response

Correct, if designed carelessly. This is why decontamination is a hardware requirement rather than a procedure, and why single-site probe assignment is the default operating posture. See biosecurity-protocol.json.

Residual Risk
Medium and manageable, but it must be engineered, and it costs mass and cycle time.
Objection

A free app is not a business. This is repositioning as a hardware company that couldn't raise money for hardware, dressed up as 'software-first.'

Our Response

The honest version of this objection has real force and should not be waved away. The response is not that the app is secretly lucrative on its own — it plainly is not, and traction.json says so honestly with real zeros — but that it is the only credible way to reach the beginner tier at all, and it produces the logged farm data that Phase 2's analytics and Phase 3's models both need and do not currently have. If the funnel from free to paid does not work, this is a weaker business than the pitch claims, and that should be said plainly rather than discovered later.

Residual Risk

High. This is the load-bearing assumption of the entire phased strategy and it is currently untested — see the freemium conversion assumptions in cost-of-inaction.json and business-model-canvas.json.

Objection

Beginners who cannot pay today will never become farms that pay tomorrow; a free tier just accumulates users who cost support time and never convert.

Our Response

Also plausible, and we do not have evidence against it. The counter-hypothesis is that some meaningful fraction of Phase 1 users grow into Phase 2 farms over multiple cycles, and that the ones who do not still generate community content and data that make the product better for those who do. Both halves of that sentence are hopes, not findings.

Residual Risk

High. Conversion rate, timescale, and support cost per free user are all unmeasured. This should be one of the first things instrumented once there are any real users at all.

What Is Established

That fish kills occur, that they cost real money, that the mechanisms are DO depletion and post-overturn toxicity, and that monitoring is inadequate at commercial scale — all supported by published sources, and all specific to the tier-3 problem. That extension services are thin and generic farm software does not serve aquaculture is a reasonable inference from the structure of the sector, not a cited finding.

What Is Not Established

Everything about tiers 1 and 2 beyond plausibility: that beginners experience the guidance gap as their top problem, that a free app is the right form for closing it, that any meaningful fraction of free users convert to paying Phase 2 customers, and — at tier 3 — that the specific operators we intend to sell hardware to experience observability as their top problem, that they would pay our price, and that they have the response capacity to act on our output. None of these are library questions. They require Phase 0 field discovery across all three tiers, not just the top one.

Intellectual Honesty Note

A problem statement built only from literature and inference is a hypothesis about a market, not evidence of one. All three tiers of this one are currently a hypothesis, and the top tier is only somewhat less so — it has more citations, not more field validation.
