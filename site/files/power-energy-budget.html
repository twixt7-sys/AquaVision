Power & Energy Budget

power-energy-budget v1.0.0 updated 2026-07-17 ◎Target ◆Confidence: low raw JSON ↗
⚠WARNING

This is a paper model built on assumed efficiencies and assumed component power draws. It has not been validated against any hardware. Its purpose is to show whether the mission profiles are plausible and to identify what must be measured. Treat the METHOD as the deliverable and every NUMBER as a placeholder.

The Governing Physics

Statement
Hovering is expensive, and our unique value requires hovering.
Explanation

A multirotor in hover burns power continuously just to stay up. Forward flight is meaningfully more efficient because the rotors work in translational lift. Our profiling missions are hover-dominated — the aircraft sits still while the winch does its slow work. So the missions that deliver our only defensible capability are also our least efficient flight mode.

Why This Is Not A Solvable Problem

It is the shape of the physics, not a design flaw. Every engineering hour spent trying to make hover cheap is better spent accepting the constraint and designing the operation around it: shorter sorties, more of them, fast dock turnaround.

The Strategic Consequence

Our endurance will always lose to a USV and a buoy. We should stop treating that as a weakness to hide and start treating it as a known trade we made deliberately in exchange for coverage and aerial imaging.

The Observation

Propulsion is roughly 92 percent of the draw. Every non-propulsion optimisation is noise. The way to buy endurance is to reduce mass or reduce hover time — nothing else moves the needle, and time spent optimising the compute module's power is time wasted.

The Caveat

This assumes hover at 720 W. If the real figure is 900 W — entirely plausible for a shrouded aircraft carrying a swinging pendulum in wind — the mission consumes 265 Wh and the margin drops to 16 percent, which is uncomfortably close to the reserve. The single most important measurement in Phase 2 is real hover power with a deployed probe in wind.

The Headline Finding
Statement

On this paper model, the mission set closes. The binding constraint is hover power, and hover power is the number we have the least confidence in.

Sensitivity

A 25 percent error in hover power turns the flagship mission from comfortable to marginal. Nothing else in the model has that leverage.

The Action

Measure hover power with a real payload on a real airframe in real wind, as early as possible. It is the number the whole concept hinges on and it is currently a guess.

Dock Energy

Recharge vs Swap
The Question
Does the dock recharge the pack in place, or swap it for a charged one?
Recharge
Pro
Simple, no mechanism, cheap dock
Con
Turnaround is measured in tens of minutes to an hour. That caps sortie cadence hard.
Swap
Pro

Minutes of turnaround. More sorties per day, which is the correct answer to short endurance.

Con
A robotic swap mechanism is its own engineering project, plus spare packs per dock.
The Reasoning

Short endurance is answered by MORE SORTIES, not longer ones. Recharge caps sorties per day at roughly the number of hours available divided by the charge time. If we want a dawn sweep, a mortality scan, a bloom survey, and an afternoon profile, recharge may not keep up — especially since the missions cluster in the solar window rather than spreading evenly.

The Recommendation

Start with recharge for Phase 2 simplicity. Model the sortie cadence honestly against charge time. If cadence binds — and the schedule in mission-profiles.json suggests it will — swap becomes necessary and it should be planned for, not discovered.

Solar Viability
Requirement

Rural sites have unreliable or absent grid power. Solar is a design condition, not an option.

The Arithmetic

Four sorties/day at ~220 Wh each is ~880 Wh of flight energy, plus charging losses, plus dock systems. Call it 1.3 kWh/day. In Philippine insolation that is a modest panel — but the monsoon is the problem, not the arithmetic. Extended overcast means the dock must carry days of storage or accept degraded cadence exactly during the season of highest risk.

The Irony

Our availability is lowest when the risk is highest, for two independent reasons: we cannot fly in the weather, and we cannot charge in the overcast. Both point the same direction, and both are honest limitations that a fixed buoy does not share.
